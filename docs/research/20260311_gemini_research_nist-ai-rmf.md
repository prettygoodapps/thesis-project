# NIST AI RMF 1.0 Analysis

## 1. Citation
National Institute of Standards and Technology. (2023). *AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0)* (NIST AI 100-1). U.S. Department of Commerce. https://doi.org/10.6028/NIST.AI.100-1

## 2. Summary
The NIST AI RMF is a voluntary framework designed to help organizations manage the risks of AI systems while promoting trustworthy development and use. It categorizes "Trustworthy AI" into seven characteristics: **Validity**, **Reliability**, **Safety**, **Security and Resiliency**, **Accountability and Transparency**, **Explainability and Interpretability**, and **Privacy-Enhanced**.

### Key Characteristics:
1. **Validity:** Correctness of outputs (predictions, classifications) relative to intended goals.
2. **Reliability:** Ability to perform as required without failure over time.
3. **Safety:** Preventing harm to humans, property, and environment.
4. **Security and Resiliency:** Protection against adversarial attacks and manipulation.
5. **Accountability and Transparency:** Clear documentation of AI lifecycle and responsibility.
6. **Explainability and Interpretability:** Understanding how outputs are reached.
7. **Privacy-Enhanced:** Minimizing data use and protecting personal information.

## 3. Methodology Assessment
- **Strengths:** High level of industry adoption. Broad scope covering technical, social, and organizational risks. Provides a common language for "Trustworthiness."
- **Weaknesses:** Voluntary and non-prescriptive, which can lead to inconsistent application. Lacks specific technical "Reliability Levels" or thresholds.

## 4. Disconfirming Evidence
- Critics argue that the framework is too generic and doesn't provide enough technical depth for specific industries like manufacturing or automotive without significant adaptation.

## 5. Thesis Relevance
- **Operational Integrity:** NIST addresses "Integrity" under the "Security and Resiliency" characteristic, focusing on protection against data poisoning and model manipulation.
- **Reliability and Validity:** These are foundational for the thesis's "Integrity Gap" argument, where AI systems can be "Reliable" (consistent) but not "Valid" (correct).
